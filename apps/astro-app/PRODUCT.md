# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

People who want to keep a complete, reliable record of what they pay without adopting a full budgeting app. Renters, homeowners, freelancers, students, and families tracking recurring household expenses. They reach for this instead of a spreadsheet, often on mobile, in a spare moment right after paying something, and they need to record it and get on with their day.

The secondary audience is small business owners who want business expenses kept separate from personal ones, served by independent profiles rather than a different product.

## Product Purpose

Smart Expense Assistant is the fastest, cleanest way to record every expense and find it again later. It answers the everyday questions a spreadsheet answers slowly: did I already pay this, how much did I spend this month, where is my money going, where is that February electricity bill. Success is a person who logs an expense in under ten seconds, comes back regularly to do it again, and stops keeping the spreadsheet.

It is deliberately not a budgeting tool, an accounting system, or a finance suite. Any summaries it shows are descriptive facts drawn from what the user recorded, never advice or prediction.

## Positioning

Excel built specifically for expenses: simple enough for anyone, with the conveniences a spreadsheet can't offer, and nothing heavier bolted on.

## Brand Personality

Calm and effortless. The interface should feel quiet and low-friction, earning trust through restraint rather than flourish. It gets out of the way so recording an expense is nearly thoughtless, and reviewing history feels like glancing at a clean ledger, not decoding a dashboard. Voice is plain and reassuring; never clever at the cost of clarity.

## Anti-references

- Complex budgeting apps (Mint / YNAB style): dense charts, budgets, and forecasts the MVP intentionally excludes.
- Cold banking and corporate fintech: navy-and-gold gravitas, intimidating number grids.
- Playful consumer fintech: loud gradients, big emoji, gamified confetti; too toy-like for a records tool.
- The raw spreadsheet it replaces: a literal grid with no hierarchy or polish.

## Design Principles

- Under ten seconds or it failed. Speed of entry is the product; every screen defends the quick path to logging an expense.
- A record, not a verdict. Show what the user paid and let them read it; describe, never advise or predict.
- Calm over clever. Restraint is the aesthetic; when in doubt, remove rather than decorate.
- Legible at a glance. History and dashboards exist to answer "did I pay this / how much" instantly, so hierarchy and scannability outrank density.
- Simpler than the spreadsheet. If a feature makes the tool feel heavier than the thing it replaces, it doesn't belong in the MVP.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA: body text at 4.5:1 contrast (large text 3:1), full keyboard navigation with visible focus states, and a reduced-motion alternative for every animation.
